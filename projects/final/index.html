Why are pop songs getting shorter?

By Abigail Olear

Friday; a music lover’s favorite day of the week.

Every Friday, numerous artists drop new singles, EPs and albums for musicphiles to analyze and add to their playlists. However, much to this demographic’s distaste, songs from their favorite artists have become shorter and shorter in recent years.

“I feel like most songs are now less than 3 minutes when they used to be pushing 4 minutes,” said sophomore journalism and cinema and media studies student Lea Glaser.

The average length of a song on Billboard’s 2023 Year-End Hot 100 was 3:13. In 1993, it was 4:19, over a minute longer.

2023’s top songs like “Ella Baila Sola,” “Kill Bill,” “Unholy,” and “Barbie World” are all less than 3 minutes long.

“Cupid - Twin Version” , another popular song of 2023, is only 2:53. There is even a sped up version of the song that shaves off another 28 seconds of the already short song.

Radios used to play nearly 6 minute long songs like “Bohemian Rhapsody” by Queen and The Beatles 7 minute long “Hey Jude.”

So, what changed?

The way in which music is consumed has affected its length.

In the 1960’s popular song length hovered around 2:30. Most people listened to music via A.M. radio. Hosts and producers preferred to play shorter songs on the radio so that if a listener did not like the song, they would know it would be over soon and not skip.

Also, shorter songs made less grooves on a record. This made the songs play on a record player louder, making them better in the ears of the listener.

The songs were also simpler, mostly following the verse, chorus, verse, bridge and final chorus structure.

In the 1970’s, ‘80’s and ‘90’s, songs steadily became longer. Vinyls became better in the ‘70’s, allowing for more songs to be included on one record. The commercial release of CDs and CD players in the early eighties allowed for even longer songs to be put on an album.

The songs also became more complex. The intro and outros of songs were longer and artists began to experiment more with bridges, pre choruses and other ‘optional’ sections of songs.

Song length peaked in the 1990’s with an average length of 3:47 in the decade.

From the early 2000’s onward, song length continued to decline. The popularity of music streaming services began in 2005 and steadily increased over the years.

Sites like Apple Music and Spotify pay an artist royalties if a listener listens to at least 30 seconds of the song. Similarly to the 1960’s, this incentivises musicians to get to the hook or chorus of their song quickly in order to keep the listener engaged.

TikTok has also had a hand in the shortening of music. Since the app’s release in 2018, the way listeners consume and judge music has significantly changed. Listeners want to be immediately hooked by the song, causing artists to get to the chorus quicker and cut out any ‘boring’ parts of the song.

Overall, income from a song has affected its length. As the music industry continues to become more and more commercialized, should song length be sacrificed?
